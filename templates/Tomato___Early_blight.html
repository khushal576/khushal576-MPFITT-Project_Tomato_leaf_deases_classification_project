What is it?

Tomato Early Blight is a fungal disease caused by the fungus Alternaria solani. It appears as dark, concentric rings with a target-like appearance on the leaves. This disease usually starts at the bottom of the plant and works its way up, causing defoliation if severe.

Why does it occur?

Tomato Early Blight is favored by warm, humid conditions. The fungus can survive in plant debris and soil and infects the plants through splashing water, wind, or contaminated tools.

Prevention:

  • Plant Resistant Varieties: Choose tomato varieties that are resistant to early blight if available in your area.
  • Crop Rotation: Rotate tomatoes with unrelated crops to reduce the buildup of fungal spores in the soil.
  • Avoid Overhead Watering: Water the plants at the base to avoid wetting the foliage, which can help prevent the spread of spores.
  • Remove Infected Plant Debris: Clean up and remove any infected plant debris from the garden to reduce the source of fungal spores.
  • Apply Mulch: Mulching around the plants can help prevent soil splashback onto the foliage.
  • Prune Lower Leaves: Remove lower leaves that may come into contact with the soil to reduce the risk of infection.

Treatment:

While there's no cure for Tomato Early Blight, you can take steps to manage the disease:

  • Remove Infected Leaves: Prune and remove infected leaves to prevent the spread of the disease.
  • Fungicidal Sprays: Apply fungicides containing chlorothalonil or copper to help control the spread of the fungus. Follow label instructions carefully.
  • Improve Air Circulation: Proper spacing between plants and adequate ventilation can help reduce humidity and minimize fungal growth.
  • Avoid Water Stress: Maintain consistent watering practices to keep the plants healthy and less susceptible to disease.
  • Monitor Regularly: Regularly inspect tomato plants for signs of early blight and take prompt action if symptoms appear.
